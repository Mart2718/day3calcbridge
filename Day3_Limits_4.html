The Problem

Calculus is how mathematics
makes sense of infinity.

Infinity is not a number you can reach — it is a direction you can travel. And calculus is the first branch of mathematics built entirely around that idea. Every major concept you will encounter this week involves infinity in disguise:

The derivative asks what happens as an interval shrinks to infinitely small. The integral asks what happens when you add infinitely many pieces. Continuity asks whether a function behaves consistently across an infinite collection of points.

All three of those questions are answered the same way — with a limit. The limit is the tool calculus invented to handle infinity precisely, without ever having to touch it directly.

A note on infinity: ∞ is not a destination — it is a process. When we write h → 0 or x → ∞, we are describing an infinite journey, not an arrival. The limit tells us what that journey approaches — and that answer is always a finite, exact number. Infinity in. Clarity out. That is what limits do.
limx → a f(x) = L
"As x gets arbitrarily close to a,
f(x) gets arbitrarily close to L."

x never has to equal a.
f(x) never has to reach L.
The approach is enough.
The Role of Infinity in Calculus
∞ is not a number — it is a direction. Limits are how calculus uses that direction to produce exact, finite answers.

h → 0  ·  x → ∞  ·  n → ∞
Infinite process. Finite result. Every time.
See It First

Three ways to feel a limit before you define one.

Visual 1
The Desert Horizon
You drive toward the desert horizon and never arrive. It recedes at exactly the rate you approach. And yet — you know exactly where it is. You can point to it. You can measure the angle to it. That is a limit. The destination is real even though you never reach it.
Visual 2
Does 0.999… equal 1?
0.999… = 1?
Raise your hand — do you believe it?
1
Let x = 0.999…
Name the repeating decimal
2
10x = 9.999…
Multiply both sides by 10
3
10x − x = 9.999… − 0.999…
Subtract the original equation
4
9x = 9
The repeating parts cancel exactly
5
x = 1
Therefore 0.999… = 1 ✓
0.999… = 1 — exactly, not approximately. An infinite process can have a finite, exact destination. That is a limit.
Zeno's Paradox — Same Idea
Achilles takes ½ the remaining distance, then ½ again, forever. An infinite number of steps — yet he finishes the race.
½ + ¼ + ⅛ + ¹⁄₁₆ + … = 1
Infinite steps. Finite destination. The limit exists.
Visual 3
Zoom Into y = x² Until It Looks Like a Line
Try it yourself — live below. Zoom into the curve at x = 1. Keep zooming. Watch what happens to the curve.
1
The graph shows y = x². Find the point (1, 1).
2
Pinch or scroll to zoom in at x = 1. Keep zooming.
3
At some point the curve becomes indistinguishable from a straight line.
4
That line has slope 2. That is the derivative — and that zoom is a limit.
As you zoom in, the curve approaches a straight line — but it never actually becomes one. The tangent line is the limit of the curve's behavior as your window shrinks to a single point. You just watched a limit happen in real time.
The Essential Question

Can you arrive somewhere
you never actually reach?

The horizon never arrives — but you know exactly where it is. 0.999… never becomes 1 in any single step — but it equals 1 exactly. The curve never becomes a line — but it approaches one so closely that calculus can name it. All three are the same idea. The limit is real even when the destination is not.

The Limit Definition
limx→a f(x) = L means f(x) can be made arbitrarily close to L by taking x close enough to a. x never has to equal a.
↔️
One-Sided Limits
A limit can be approached from the left or right. If both sides agree, the limit exists. If they disagree — it doesn't. Both outcomes matter.
🔗
Limits Power Everything
The derivative from Day 1 and Day 2 is a limit in disguise. limh→0 [f(a+h)−f(a)]/h — now you know what that arrow actually means.
